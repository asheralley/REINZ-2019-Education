Question 1

Please answer in your downloaded booklet

A boundary being in bushland, or other indications that the boundary is not clear, should put licensees on notice that their obligation to check boundary details is extended. What should the licensee have done in this case to avoid a finding of unsatisfactory conduct?

Question 1

Answer reference:

Answer should make reference to the following:
‘…In these circumstances the licensee has a positive obligation to ensure that any marketing material that he or she prepares which might indicate a boundary where the licensee is not certain of the boundaries clearly reflects the fact that that particular part of the property is either not within the boundaries of the property, or that the licensee is uncertain as to where the boundaries are.
‘…A licensee is not required to be a surveyor but in cases like this; where the front boundary, was not where it appeared to be, and the back boundary was in bush we consider that the licensee should have been on notice that a disclaimer [relating to the boundaries] may have been required in the marketing material.
‘Alternatively, he should have required the vendor to provide information as to the position of the boundaries prior to the marketing campaign beginning. A licensee must at least consider where the boundaries are, especially where part of the boundary was in bush.’

Video 2

Question 2

Please answer in your downloaded booklet

While a due diligence clause covering investigation of the location of the boundaries of the land was included in the sale and purchase agreement, the CAC confirmed that the clause did not abdicate the licensee from his obligations to be certain that the advice and representations he had given were correct.
What should the licensee have done in this case to avoid a finding of unsatisfactory conduct?

Question 2

Answer reference:

Answer should make reference to the following:
The CAC said that because of the condition of the lot (with long grass, no plan showing the actual lots and survey marker pegs not visible) the licensee should have approached the advice he was giving whether it be general or specific with caution.
The licensee should have advised the complainants that the information he was providing would need to be clarified once he was able to view the subdivision plan and the property file back at his office.
The CAC said that at the time of drawing up the sale and purchase agreement, the licensee should have confirmed it related to the correct lot.

Video 3

Question 3

Please answer in your downloaded booklet

Despite providing material documentation, the licensee was found to have ‘selectively withheld’ information from the complainant. Which information that wasn’t disclosed should she have given to the complainant in relation to property defects?

Question 3

Answer reference:

Answer should make reference to the following:
The CAC said that the licensee had ‘selectively withheld’ information from the complainant which in fairness should have been disclosed.
The CAC said that while it would have been reasonable for the licensee to have believed she could rely on the engineer’s report, she should have fully disclosed the history of the property and the buy-back to the complainant.

Video 4

Question 4

Please answer in your downloaded booklet

In terms of the issue with asbestos cladding and communication with the vendor and prospective purchasers, what should the licensee have done to avoid a finding of unsatisfactory conduct?

Question 4

Answer reference:

Answer should make reference to the following:
The CAC said ‘if a licensee suspects cladding or any other material used in the construction of a dwelling might contain asbestos, then this is a matter that must be discussed with a vendor client before marketing a property. It is also a matter that must in all fairness be disclosed to a purchaser prior to an agreement for sale and purchase being signed…’
‘The obvious course of action would have been for the complainant to instruct the licensee to disclose that possibility to any prospective purchaser whereby the prospective purchaser could take further advice and make an informed decision about whether or not to continue with the purchase. Alternatively, had the complainant been advised by the licensee of the possibility of asbestos in the cladding at the time of listing and instructed the licensee not to disclose this defect to purchasers, the licensee would have been required under rule 10.8 to terminate the agency agreement…’
